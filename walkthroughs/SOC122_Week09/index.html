When Power Feels Normal

The people in this lobby appear to be making individual choices, yet the room has already organized what can happen. Portraits suggest an approved identity. Signals rank access. Separate counters turn judgment into routine. This is the bridge from Althusser to Number Twelve Looks Just Like You and Nosedive: power can shape people by teaching them what normal looks like.

Where This Week Sits

This live week moves from sociology's history into ideology, normalization, stratification, social influence, and power. Althusser helps explain how institutions make social roles feel natural. Number Twelve Looks Just Like You turns an approved body into a social requirement, while Nosedive turns public approval into a rating tied to housing, service, mobility, and status.

Insight

The central question is: How do institutions and everyday interactions make hierarchy feel normal, voluntary, or deserved?

Through Two-Eyed Seeing

OpenStax and Althusser explain stratification, institutions, ideology, and social reproduction within Western sociology. Pamela Palmater, who is Mi'kmaw, locates First Nations poverty in Canada's colonial legal and policy order rather than in individual or cultural failure. The sources are not interchangeable. Holding them together shows that inequality is measured, interpreted, normalized, and produced through specific histories and structures.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

The Week in Three Moves

Move one uses OpenStax and Althusser to locate stratification, ideology, institutions, and interpellation. Move two compares Number Twelve Looks Just Like You with Nosedive: an approved body becomes an approved public self, and social approval becomes material access. Move three reads Palmater and one Canadian inequality measure to ask what the number shows, what the legal history explains, and what neither source should be made to say alone.

Insight

The week is built as a path: set the context, work with sources, then apply the idea in a bounded way.

Then place one inequality statistic beside Palmater: write what the number shows, what the historical and legal account explains, and what question remains.

What You Are Learning This Week

Four key takeaways from this walkthrough
1

Trace How Power Becomes Normal

Institutions, media, categories, ratings, and everyday interactions can make hierarchy feel voluntary or deserved.

2

Keep the Sources Distinct

OpenStax and Althusser explain stratification and ideological reproduction. Palmater names the Canadian colonial law and policy that produced a specific inequality.

3

Use the Sources Carefully

The readings are named here for orientation, but the full texts stay on Blackboard or their approved access paths.

4

Turn the Week Into Practice

Audit one media scene and one inequality measure: identify the standard, mechanism, consequence, evidence, and remaining question.
