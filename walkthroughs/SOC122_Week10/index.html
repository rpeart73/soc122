Week 10: Psychology Part 1
Fall 2026

Who Defines Normal?

The observation room combines older instruments, clinical space, records, and a Canadian public-health context. Psychology created powerful ways to study experience and behaviour. It also helped institutions classify, treat, and sometimes confine people. Mental health cannot be understood only as an individual condition; stigma, access, history, culture, and social inequality shape what people experience and whether they are believed.

What can each tool measure, and what might it leave out?

Where This Week Sits

This week begins with the history of Western psychology and its changing definitions of mind, behaviour, evidence, normality, and mental health. It then moves into public life: Nightmare at 20,000 Feet asks how a recent mental-health crisis changes Bob Wilson's credibility; the CAMH Queen Street site records a Canadian history of asylum, patient labour, changing language, care, and patient voice; current Canadian evidence keeps social and structural conditions inside the explanation.

Insight

The central question is: Who gets defined, who gets believed, and which conditions count as part of mental health?

Think about it

No one is asked to diagnose themselves, diagnose another person, or disclose a personal mental-health experience. A fictional, historical, institutional, or public-policy example is the expected route.

What You Are Learning This Week

Four key takeaways from this walkthrough
1

Historicize the Discipline

Psychology changed what it studied, how it produced evidence, and what it called normal.

2

Keep the Accounts Distinct

Western disciplinary history, Canadian institutional and public-health evidence, and Gone's Indigenous account each make different claims.

3

Use the Sources Carefully

The readings are named here for orientation, but the full texts stay on Blackboard or their approved access paths.

4

Turn the Week Into Practice

Audit one public source for its problem, evidence, authority, social context, and limit, with no personal disclosure required.
