Audit the Experiment

The corridor does not tell us that people naturally became prisoners or guards. It asks us to inspect the setting: who designed the roles, what participants were told, which behaviours were rewarded, what the observer could see, and when the study should have stopped. Le Texier's archival critique makes the Stanford Prison Experiment a lesson in research design, evidence, power, and the afterlife of a famous story.

Look closely

Which parts of the environment were treated as neutral even though researchers created them?

Research rule

Fame is not evidence. Compare the original claim, the archival record, the design, and the story repeated afterward.

Research designDemand characteristicsEthics

Start with the designed environment. Then ask students to distinguish what was observed, what was instructed, what was inferred, and what later retellings exaggerated.

Where This Week Sits

This asynchronous week turns from psychology's history to the power of situations, roles, researchers, institutions, and public stories. The Stanford Prison Experiment is not taught as a settled fact about human nature. Students examine the archive and Le Texier's critique to ask how instructions, role performance, researcher authority, ethics, selective reporting, and later retelling shaped what the study became.

Insight

The central question is: What can a famous experiment support after we include the power held by the researchers and the institution?

Think about it

Was the mock prison a neutral situation, or did the research team help script the roles?

Through Two-Eyed Seeing

OpenStax, the Stanford archive, and Le Texier let students audit a Western social-psychology claim, its design, and its cultural afterlife. Bombay and colleagues examine the intergenerational effects of Indian Residential Schools through a different history, population, and evidence base. The cases must not be equated. Holding them side by side teaches why context, power, evidence, and historical specificity matter.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

Think about it

The Week in Three Moves

Move one identifies the public Stanford story and the claim usually made about roles and situations. Move two examines the archive, researcher instructions, demand characteristics, stopping decisions, ethics, and selective reporting. Move three uses Le Texier to test the strength of the evidence, then places the audit beside Bombay without merging unlike histories. Students finish by stating one claim the Stanford evidence can support and one it cannot.

Use the Bombay reading in a separate column so its historical and community-specific account is never turned into a laboratory comparison.

What You Are Learning This Week

Four key takeaways from this walkthrough
1

Treat the Famous Study as a Claim

The Stanford story must be tested against its design, researcher influence, ethics, archive, and later criticism.

2

Do Not Equate Unlike Cases

The Stanford audit and Bombay's account have different histories, purposes, populations, methods, and responsibilities.

3

Use the Sources Carefully

The readings are named here for orientation, but the full texts stay on Blackboard or their approved access paths.

4

Turn the Week Into Practice

Separate what the archive shows, what the researchers shaped, what later criticism challenges, and what remains uncertain.
